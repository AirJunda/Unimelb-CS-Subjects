Exercise UIC / Critiquing user interfaces

Objectives

  1. To gain experience of the many different types of interfaces we encounter every day;
  2. To critically assess the user interfaces you encounter;
  3. To gain experience with mapping and sharing geographic data with the class; and 
  4. To practice your presentation skills and team work. 

In a nutshell...

In small groups, your task is to seek out on the campus examples of good and bad user interfaces. These might be anything from on-campus maps and signage, to doors, telephones, paths, or other objects. You must take a photo of your chosen object, note its coordinates, and upload your picture along with a brief explanation of your choice to a class map.

The fine print...

You should complete this exercise in your groups of 3 or 4 people. You can then split your task into four stages.

Stage 1: Identify good and bad interfaces on campus

In your groups, you should explore the university campus and not any examples of good or bad interfaces you come across. Any type of user interface is acceptable, from maps or signage, to doors or windows, to photocopier or telephones. You should identify at least two good and two bad interface examples, preferably outside. You should use the principles of interface design covered in the lectures in determining whether you think a particular interface is good or bad.

Stage 2: Capture data about your interfaces

You will need to generate the following information about your chosen interface examples:

  • A photograph of your example: You should photograph your example interface and upload it to the course web site, giving it a suitable name. You will need to make your photo public ("Permit anyone") to make it accessible to all. Note the URL of your photo.
  • The lat/long location of your example: You will need to record the coordinate location of your example interface. It is up to you to determine how to find the coordinate location. There are many websites and apps that can help with this task.
  • Your critique: You should generate a brief summary of why you chose your interface as a good/bad example, with reference to the principles of good interface design covered in class.

Stage 3: Map your results

You must create a map of your examples of good and bad interfaces. There are a variety of different systems you can use, such as Mapbox (http://mapbox.com). You should then upload the data about your examples to your map, including:

  • A marker at the point on the campus where your example can be found;
  • A brief title indicating what the interface is and whether you chose it as a good or bad example;
  • Your image of the interface and your brief explanation of your critique within the description for the marker.

In Mapbox, there are a number of ways to upload your data, either manually from the Mapbox interface or via generating and uploading GeoJSON files. Note that you can include HTML <img> tags within your marker description.

Example:

The image below, drawn from the Mapbox map at https://a.tiles.mapbox.com/v3/mduckham.i9dajmdm/page.html#13/-35.3333/149.0676 shows an example of a particularly bad interface (a tap) in a government building in Canberra.

Stage 4: Share your findings 

You should make the URL of your map available via your group blog in order for others to see your chosen interfaces. 

Selected groups must also orally present findings to the whole class. Each group will have at most 5 minutes briefly to present either their best or worst interfaces and to summarize their analysis and justifications for the choice. You will be able to access your map (no powerpoint, no bullet points). You should be prepared to answer questions on your choices and analysis, as well as support other presenters by asking question about their choices.  

At the end, we shall hold a class vote on the overall best and worst interface.

Assessment

This exercise is not assessed.
